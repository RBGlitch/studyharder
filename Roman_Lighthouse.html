Roman Lighthouse in Dover

The octagonal Roman pharos, or lighthouse, in Dover Castle is one of three lighthouses that have survived since the Roman Empire (the other two are in Libya and Spain), and the tallest Roman structure in the UK (~24 meters). After the invasion of England in 43 AD, the Romans built a port. Two lighthouses were built on both sides of the harbor to navigate the ships. Only the eastern lighthouse has survived to this day. Dover, or Dubris, as the Romans called it, was the closest point to the European mainland. The Door river, now mostly covered over, was then an important channel of trade. The Romans built a fort where the present castle stands. The lighthouse is made of layers of tuff, Kentish rag stone and red brick. The castle lighthouse survived after being converted into a bell tower in the Saxon era (about 1000) with the addition of a new upper tier (about 1430) and partially renovated in 1913-1915.
